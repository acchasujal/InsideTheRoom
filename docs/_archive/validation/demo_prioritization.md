# Demo Prioritization — Phase 2C

**Status:** CANONICAL. This document defines what goes into the 3-minute demo, what gets cut, and what gets emphasized.

**Optimization target:** RECALL. Not completeness. The demo exists to produce 2-3 moments a judge will remember a week later. Everything else is scaffolding.

---

## DOES THE CURRENT STRUCTURE FIT 3 MINUTES?

### Original structure: 5 full decide-reveal-decide cycles

**Verdict: NO.** A full cycle (decision → evidence → 5 perspectives → undefined-term reveal → second decision → reflection) takes approximately 60-90 seconds when compressed to its minimum. Five cycles = 5-7.5 minutes. This does not fit a 3-minute demo under any pacing model.

### Reconciled structure: 4 cycles + 1 nested layer

**Verdict: TIGHT BUT FEASIBLE.** The reconciliation reduced the structure from 5 standalone cycles to 4 cycles + 1 nested layer:

1. Perišić (Intent/Threshold) → full cycle + VAR nested reveal (~70 seconds)
2. De Jong (Threshold) → full cycle (~45 seconds)
3. Mbappé (Scope) → compressed cycle (~35 seconds)
4. Suárez (Context) → full cycle with extended close (~50 seconds)

**Total: ~200 seconds = 3 minutes 20 seconds.** Still over. Requires further compression.

---

## WHAT GETS CUT

### From every incident:
- **Five-perspective display is reduced to highlight reel.** The demo does not walk through all five perspectives sequentially. It shows the *divergence* — the 2-3 perspectives that disagree most sharply — and moves to the reveal. Full perspective exploration is available in the product; the demo proves divergence exists, it doesn't enumerate it.
- **Aggregate data (how others judged) is cut from the demo.** This is a product feature, not a demo feature. In 3 minutes, the user's own shifting judgment is the proof. Showing aggregate statistics adds information without adding memorability.
- **Reflection text is cut.** The compressed insight that appears after Decision 2 in the signature moment design is powerful in the product experience but costs 5-10 seconds per incident in the demo. The reveal itself serves as the reflection.

### From specific incidents:
- **Mbappé gets the deepest compression.** It receives the minimum viable treatment: Decision 1 → brief evidence ("García touched the ball — was it deliberate or a deflection?") → reveal ("'deliberately played' has no definition, and neither do the guidance terms used to define it") → Decision 2. The "undefined all the way down" insight is delivered in one sentence, not a build-up. Target: 35 seconds.
- **VAR Judgment loses its own Decision cycle entirely.** As a nested layer inside Perišić, it gets: one question ("Should VAR have intervened — was the original no-penalty call a 'clear and obvious error'?") → one reveal ("'clear and obvious' has no definition either") → one compressed insight ("the system built to fix the problem has the problem"). No Decision 1 / Decision 2 structure for VAR. Target: 20 seconds.

---

## WHAT GETS SIMPLIFIED

| Element | Full Product Version | Demo Version |
|:--------|:--------------------|:-------------|
| Perspectives | 5 full perspectives with citations | 2-3 key disagreements, headline-level |
| Evidence | Full match context, footage description, aftermath | One-line setup, jump to the controversy |
| Legal text | Full Law 12 excerpt with highlighting | The undefined term + one sentence of surrounding law |
| Aggregate data | Full distribution of user judgments | Cut entirely from demo |
| Reflection text | Compressed insight paragraph | The reveal IS the reflection |
| Category labels | Full taxonomy explanation | Brief label on-screen; taxonomy is implicit, not taught |

---

## WHAT GETS EMPHASIZED

### The three beats that must land:

**Beat 1: Perišić reveal.** "The word 'deliberately' appears four times in Law 12 and has no definition." This is the thesis made concrete. If the judge remembers nothing else, they remember this.

**Beat 2: De Jong proof.** "The referee gave yellow. Years later, watching the same footage, he said it should have been red." This is the single most quotable evidence. It eliminates the "referees just disagree" counter-argument.

**Beat 3: Suárez closer.** "Every rule was followed. Ghana still went home." This is the emotional climax. It's what survives a week in judge memory. It requires zero football literacy.

### If only one beat lands:
**Suárez.** It is the most accessible (no rules knowledge needed), the most emotionally charged (moral outrage without nuance), and the most concisely quotable ("the law was followed perfectly and the outcome was still unjust"). If the demo runs long and everything is rushed, the Suárez closing must still be delivered at full pace with full weight.

## SECOND-BY-SECOND TIME BUDGET

| Time | Content | Purpose |
|:-----|:--------|:--------|
| **0:00–0:10** | Hook / thesis statement | "Football's biggest controversies aren't about what happened. They're about what a word means." |
| **0:10–0:20** | Perišić setup | World Cup Final, 2018. Ball hits Perišić's hand. User asked: "Penalty?" |
| **0:20–0:30** | Perišić Decision 1 + key perspective divergence | Show 2-3 opposing views, headline-level. Fan says penalty. Player says natural position. |
| **0:30–0:45** | Perišić reveal | "Deliberately" has no definition. "Unnaturally bigger" has no definition. Two undefined terms decided a World Cup Final. Decision 2. |
| **0:45–1:05** | VAR nested layer | "The referee initially said no penalty. VAR told him to look again. But VAR can only act for a 'clear and obvious error.' That phrase has no definition either. The correction mechanism has the same problem." |
| **1:05–1:15** | De Jong setup | World Cup Final, 2010. Studs to chest. User asked: "What card?" |
| **1:15–1:30** | De Jong Decision 1 + Webb proof | Show the kick. Most users: red card. Reveal: Webb gave yellow. Reveal: Webb later said red. Same referee, same footage, two answers. |
| **1:30–1:45** | De Jong reveal + Decision 2 | "Reckless" vs. "excessive force" — no defined boundary. The world's best referee couldn't consistently apply it to his own decision. |
| **1:45–2:00** | Mbappé compressed | Mbappé offside? García touched the ball. "Deliberately played" resets offside. But "deliberately played" has no definition. Its own guidance terms have no definitions. Undefined all the way down. Decision 1 → reveal → Decision 2. |
| **2:00–2:10** | Transition to Suárez | Tone shift. "The first three incidents showed you terms the law uses but doesn't define. This last one is different." |
| **2:10–2:25** | Suárez setup + Decision 1 | 2010 World Cup. Ghana about to reach the semi-final. Suárez punches the ball off the line. "Was justice served?" |
| **2:25–2:45** | Suárez reveal | Red card: correct. Penalty: correct. Every rule followed. Ghana missed the penalty. Suárez celebrated. Uruguay advanced. "The law doesn't define 'adequate remedy.' It doesn't define 'justice.' It defines procedure." |
| **2:45–3:00** | Close | "Football law doesn't need better data. It was designed to need better judgment. And it put that judgment in a room — the VAR room — where the people making the call are using words that have no definitions. That's what we built." |

**Total: 180 seconds = 3:00.**

## RUTHLESS CUT HIERARCHY

If the demo runs long, cut in this order:

| Priority | What Gets Cut | Time Saved | What's Lost |
|:---------|:-------------|:-----------|:------------|
| 1st | Mbappé entirely | ~15 seconds | Scope category, "undefined all the way down" insight |
| 2nd | VAR nested layer | ~20 seconds | Judgment category, recursive-correction insight |
| 3rd | De Jong Decision 2 | ~5 seconds | Second judgment moment (keep the Webb proof) |
| 4th | De Jong entirely | ~25 seconds | Threshold category, "same referee" proof |
| **NEVER CUT** | Perišić reveal | — | The thesis's primary proof |
| **NEVER CUT** | Suárez closer | — | The demo's closing beat; the thing that wins |

**Minimum viable demo (if catastrophically time-constrained): Perišić + Suárez only.**

Even a two-incident demo — "a word with no definition decided a World Cup Final" + "every rule was followed and the outcome was still unjust" — delivers the thesis and the emotional close. Everything between is strengthening, not essential.

## WHAT THE DEMO IS OPTIMIZED FOR

| Optimize For | How |
|:------------|:----|
| **Recall** | Three quotable beats (Perišić term, De Jong reversal, Suárez closer). A judge needs to carry at most one of these out of the room. |
| **Participation** | User makes real decisions (penalty/no penalty, what card, was justice served). They are a participant, not a viewer. |
| **Surprise** | The undefined-term reveal is the structural surprise. It arrives after the user has committed to a confident judgment. |
| **Emotional arc** | Opens with intellectual destabilization (Perišić), escalates through visceral impact (De Jong), builds philosophical depth (Mbappé), closes with moral weight (Suárez). |
| **Memorability** | The Suárez closing line is designed to be the last thing a judge hears. It survives a week. |

| Do NOT Optimize For | Why |
|:-------------------|:----|
| **Completeness** | Showing all five categories fully is less important than landing any single beat perfectly. |
| **Feature demonstration** | The demo proves a thesis, not a feature list. Judges remember arguments, not capabilities. |
| **Technical depth** | Granite's structural necessity is shown implicitly (perspectives diverge on the same evidence) not explicitly (no "here's our architecture" slide). |
| **Fairness to all incidents** | If time is short, Mbappé is sacrificed first. Not every incident gets equal weight. The demo is not a survey — it is a narrative. |
