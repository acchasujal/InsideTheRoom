### 1. INTENT — Perišić Handball (2018 World Cup Final)

**Match:** France vs. Croatia, World Cup Final, 2018
**FIFA Law:** Law 12, Section 1 — Handling the ball
**Undefined Term:** "Deliberately" / "Unnaturally bigger"
**Outcome:** Penalty awarded via VAR → Griezmann scores → France leads 2-1 → France wins 4-2

#### Why It Survived

This is the single strongest thesis incident in the entire universe. Every condition is met:

1. **Complete data exists.** Multiple camera angles. Slow motion. Frame-by-frame. Nothing is hidden. Nothing is ambiguous about *what happened*.
2. **The controversy is 100% interpretive.** Experts who saw identical footage reached opposite conclusions. Some said natural jumping motion. Others said arm extended unnaturally. The disagreement is about the *meaning of a term*, not the facts of the event.
3. **The undefined term is provably undefined.** "Deliberately" has no formal definition in the Laws of the Game. "Unnaturally bigger" has no measurable standard. The Laws say the arm position must be "a consequence of, or justifiable by, the player's body movement for that specific situation" — but "justifiable" is itself a judgment term.
4. **It's a World Cup Final.** Maximum stakes. Maximum memorability. Maximum emotional weight.
5. **VAR was used.** The referee initially said no foul. Technology made him review. He changed his mind. The technology provided more *data* but did not resolve the *interpretation*.
6. **It invites participation.** Any viewer can look at Perišić's arm and form an opinion. That opinion will genuinely vary. The user becomes a participant, not a spectator.

#### Why Alternatives Were Rejected

- **Maradona (1986):** Most iconic handball in history, but thesis-toxic. The handball was unambiguously deliberate once seen. The controversy was observational failure, not interpretive ambiguity. Using it would *disprove* the thesis — VAR would have resolved it instantly.
- **Henry (2009):** Strong emotional resonance, but weakened by two factors: (1) Rule evolution has reduced the centrality of "deliberate" — the IFAB now penalizes attackers who handle regardless of intent. (2) The double touch was so blatant that "deliberate" was barely disputed; the real controversy was about replay/justice, not legal interpretation.
- **Totti Simulation (2002):** Strong intent-ambiguity case, but lower memorability and visual clarity. The simulation/foul distinction is philosophically rich but harder to present — it requires more context setup than Perišić, which is instantly legible.

#### Why This Incident Helps Win

A judge will look at the Perišić clip and *form their own opinion* within seconds. They will feel certain. Then they will see the FIFA Law text: "deliberately handles the ball." Then they will see: *"deliberately" has no formal definition.* Then they will realize: the certainty they felt was based on a word that means whatever they want it to mean. That cognitive destabilization IS the thesis.

### 2. THRESHOLD — De Jong Chest Kick on Xabi Alonso (2010 World Cup Final)

**Match:** Netherlands vs. Spain, World Cup Final, 2010
**FIFA Law:** Law 12, Section 3 — Disciplinary action
**Undefined Term:** "Serious foul play" vs. "reckless" (the boundary between yellow and red)
**Outcome:** Yellow card given → Webb later admits it should have been red

#### Why It Survived

1. **The visual is unambiguous and instantly shocking.** Studs to chest. No debate about what happened.
2. **The controversy is purely about threshold.** Everyone agrees it was a foul. The only question: was it "reckless" (yellow) or "serious foul play" (red)?
3. **The undefined boundary is the entire dispute.** The Laws define "reckless" as "disregard to danger" and "excessive force" as "using excessive force or brutality." Where one ends and the other begins is not defined. There is no measurable criterion — no force threshold, no height threshold, no injury threshold.
4. **The referee himself changed his mind.** Howard Webb — one of the world's best referees — applied the threshold one way during the match and the opposite way after reviewing the same footage. This is the strongest possible proof that the threshold is undefined: the same expert, same evidence, two different conclusions.
5. **World Cup Final stakes.** In the most important match of the tournament, the most important tactical question (10 men vs. 11) turned on a word without a definition.

#### Why Alternatives Were Rejected

- **Schumacher vs. Battiston (1982):** More extreme violence, but too historically distant (1982 footage quality, no modern technology comparison possible, different era of refereeing). Also, the failure was so extreme (no foul at all) that it reads more as incompetence than interpretive ambiguity.
- **Robben vs. Mexico (2014):** Strong threshold case for "how much contact = a foul," but the cultural associations ("No Era Penal" meme) risk trivializing the legal point. Also, the threshold question (enough contact?) is less visually dramatic than "yellow or red for a chest kick."

#### Why This Incident Helps Win

The user sees the kick. They immediately think: "That's a red card." Or maybe: "Yellow — he was going for the ball." Either way, they have *certainty*. Then they see that the referee — one of the best in the world — gave a yellow. Then they see the law: "serious foul play = excessive force endangering safety." Then they see: the law does not define where "reckless" ends and "excessive force" begins. Then they learn: the referee later said he was wrong. The punchline: if the referee who *saw it live* can't consistently apply the threshold, the threshold doesn't exist in any operational sense.

### 3. JUDGMENT — VAR "Clear and Obvious Error" (Systemic, 2018-2026)

**Match:** Pattern across multiple incidents (best instantiated through a specific example during demo, e.g., France-Senegal 2026 or the 2018 Final Perišić review itself)
**FIFA Law:** VAR Protocol — Intervention threshold
**Undefined Term:** "Clear and obvious error"
**Outcome:** Systemic disagreement about when VAR should and should not intervene

#### Why It Survived

1. **This is the purest thesis proof in the entire set.** The correction mechanism itself — the tool *designed to reduce interpretive error* — uses an undefined term to define its own activation threshold.
2. **It creates a meta-layer of irresolvable ambiguity.** "Clear and obvious" is a judgment about whether another judgment was wrong enough to correct. It is subjective assessment of the subjectivity of another assessment. No data resolves this because the question is definitional, not factual.
3. **It is maximally relevant to the competition context.** VAR is the most discussed topic in football governance. Every fan has an opinion on whether VAR "works." This incident connects the thesis to the #1 conversation in the sport.
4. **It demonstrates the law functioning as designed.** VAR's "clear and obvious" threshold isn't a failure — it's an *intentional* design choice to preserve referee authority while adding a correction mechanism. The ambiguity is architectural.

#### Why Alternatives Were Rejected

- **France-Senegal (2026):** Strong individual instantiation of the "clear and obvious" problem, but too recent — may lack established consensus and documentation. Better used as a supporting example within the VAR pattern rather than the primary incident.
- No other incidents competed for the Judgment category. This systemic pattern is unique and irreplaceable.

#### Why This Incident Helps Win

This incident directly counters the DECODED thesis. DECODED's premise: data reveals truth. VAR is the ultimate test of that premise — it provides maximum data to correct errors. But VAR's own activation requires a *judgment* about whether an error is "clear and obvious." **The technology designed to eliminate judgment errors requires a judgment to activate.** This is the thesis at its most elegant. A judge who encounters this realizes: even the correction layer is interpretive. There is no escape from judgment.

> **Implementation note:** This incident requires a concrete instantiation for the demo. The Perišić handball can serve double duty — it is both an Intent incident AND a Judgment incident (the VAR review itself was an application of "clear and obvious error"). The demo could show the incident first as an Intent question, then reveal it was reviewed under the "clear and obvious" standard, then reveal that standard is itself undefined.

### 4. SCOPE — Mbappé "Deliberate Play" Offside Reset (2021 Nations League Final)

**Match:** France vs. Spain, UEFA Nations League Final, 2021
**FIFA Law:** Law 11 — Offside / "Deliberately played"
**Undefined Term:** "Deliberately played" (as distinguished from deflection)
**Outcome:** Goal awarded to Mbappé — García's touch ruled as "deliberate play" resetting offside

#### Why It Survived

1. **The scope term is the decision.** Whether García "deliberately played" the ball determines whether Mbappé is offside. If it's a deliberate play, offside resets. If it's a deflection, Mbappé is offside and the goal is disallowed. The entire outcome hinges on the scope of one term.
2. **The law's own guidance is itself undefined.** The IFAB guidance says "deliberately played" requires the player to have a "clear view of the ball," the ball "not moving quickly," and "time to coordinate body movement." But what constitutes a "clear view"? How fast is "not quickly"? How much time is "enough to coordinate"? The guidance terms are as undefined as the term they're trying to define.
3. **The visual is genuinely ambiguous.** Was García reaching for the ball (deliberate play) or reflexively stretching (deflection)? Reasonable viewers will disagree, and their disagreement will be based on different interpretations of the same footage.
4. **It's a final.** Nations League Final adds stakes and memorability.

#### Why Alternatives Were Rejected

- **Argentina Substitutes on Pitch (2022):** Weaker visual moment. The "scope" question (did they interfere?) is less dramatic and harder to present. The incident is also less well-known and the scope question is less central to the outcome (other factors were more salient in the controversy).
- **Lautaro Offside Check (2022):** Resolved by semi-automated technology — the specific case was determined to be onside by measurement. Weak thesis case for this particular instance, even though the broader "interfering with play" question remains interpretive.

#### Why This Incident Helps Win

The user sees Mbappé score. They see García touch the ball. They are asked: "Did García deliberately play the ball, or was it a deflection?" They choose. Then they see the FIFA law: offside is only an offense if the ball was not "deliberately played" by an opponent. Then they see the guidance for "deliberately played": "clear view... ball not moving quickly... time to coordinate." Then they see: none of these guidance terms have formal definitions either. **The scope of the law depends on a term whose own definition uses undefined terms.** It's undefined all the way down.

### 5. CONTEXT — Suárez Goal-Line Handball vs. Ghana (2010 World Cup Quarter-Final)

**Match:** Uruguay vs. Ghana, World Cup Quarter-Final, 2010
**FIFA Law:** Law 12 — Handling / DOGSO; Law 5 — Advantage
**Undefined Term:** Contextual adequacy of prescribed punishment / advantage application scope
**Outcome:** Red card + penalty correctly applied → Ghana misses penalty → Uruguay advances

#### Why It Survived

1. **The law was correctly applied — and the outcome still felt unjust.** This is the purest context-ambiguity case. The referee did everything right. Red card for DOGSO. Penalty awarded. Rules followed. And yet the outcome was widely perceived as a miscarriage of justice because the offender benefited strategically from breaking the law.
2. **The controversy is about what the law doesn't say, not what it says wrong.** The law doesn't say "if a player deliberately prevents a goal, award the goal." It prescribes a penalty kick, which is a *chance* at a goal, not a guarantee. The gap between the law's remedy and the situation's moral demand is the undefined element.
3. **Maximum emotional impact.** Ghana — potentially the first African nation in a World Cup semi-final — eliminated because a player cheated, was punished correctly, and still benefited. Suárez's celebration after Gyan missed the penalty is one of the most visceral images in World Cup history.
4. **It invites genuine moral disagreement.** Was Suárez wrong? Or was he strategically rational within the law's constraints? The law provides no framework for judging the morality of strategic rule-violation. This is context the law doesn't enumerate.
5. **It demonstrates the thesis at its most profound.** The issue isn't that the law needs better words. The issue is that the law *cannot* enumerate every context in which its remedies might be inadequate. This is law functioning as a system with inherent incompleteness.

#### Why Alternatives Were Rejected

- **Zidane Headbutt (2006):** Higher memorability (TOTAL 69 vs. 71 — close). But the thesis demonstration is weaker. The red card was straightforward. The controversy about provocation, while genuine, is more about *ethics* than about *law*. The law clearly says violent conduct = red card. The undefined element (should provocation mitigate?) is real but less structurally compelling than Suárez, where the law was perfectly applied and the outcome was still perceived as unjust.
- **Zidane also has a thesis risk:** a skeptical judge could argue "the law worked — violent conduct was punished." With Suárez, the response to "the law worked" is: "yes, exactly — and the outcome was still unjust, which is the point."

#### Why This Incident Helps Win

The user judges: "Should Suárez be punished?" Almost everyone says yes. The reveal: "He was punished. Red card. Penalty. Every rule was followed." The second reveal: "But Ghana still lost. The penalty was missed. Suárez celebrated. Uruguay advanced." The final question: "Was justice served?" The law says yes. The viewer's gut says no. The undefined element: the law does not define what constitutes adequate remedy for strategic rule-violation. **The controversy is not about what happened. It is about whether the law's prescribed remedy matches the context's moral demand — and the law is silent on this question.**
