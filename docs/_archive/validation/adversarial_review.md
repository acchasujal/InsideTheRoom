# Adversarial Review — Phase 2B

**Role:** Skeptical final-round judge. The prior four documents are treated as the work of a competent strategist who got most of it right. This document exists to find the 20% that isn't right, because a judge will find it if I don't.

---

## TASK 1 — ATTACK THE FINAL FIVE

### 1. Perišić Handball (Intent)

**Strengths.** All footage exists; the dispute is provably about meaning, not facts. Maximum stakes (World Cup Final). Easiest of the five to render as a clean two-option decision.

**Weaknesses.**

The incident is mis-categorized. "Deliberately" is genuinely an intent term, but the actual controversy — almost universally, in real commentary — was about "unnaturally bigger," i.e., how far an arm can extend during a jump before it counts. That is a severity/threshold question, not a mental-state question. Nobody seriously argued Perišić consciously chose to catch the ball; the argument was about how much arm extension is "natural." This incident is doing Threshold work while wearing an Intent label. The project's own stated strength — "each category proves the thesis through a different mechanism, no two incidents make the same argument" — is the exact claim this conflation undermines. A judge who notices it gets to say: you have four categories, not five, and your flagship incident is in the wrong one.

There's a contamination problem with the two-decision mechanic itself: this is one of the most replayed, most-discussed handball decisions in World Cup history. A meaningful share of users will recall the outcome (penalty given, France scored, France won) before they "decide" anything. The "decision before knowledge" sequence the whole product depends on is weakest exactly on the incident chosen to carry it.

There's also a framing risk specific to this incident: VAR did intervene and did produce a ruling. A skeptic can read that as the system working, not failing — "the technology caught what the eye missed" is a perfectly coherent alternative reading of the same event, and it directly supports the competitor thesis ("data reveals truth") rather than this project's thesis. The incident has to be steered carefully to avoid accidentally proving the opposing argument.

**Could a judge reasonably reject this incident? Yes.** On category-fit grounds alone (Intent vs. Threshold conflation), and on the "VAR worked" alternative reading.

### 2. De Jong Chest Kick (Threshold)

**Strengths.** The single best piece of evidence in the entire incident set: the same referee, same footage, two opposite conclusions. This is harder to argue around than any "experts disagreed" framing because it removes inter-rater variance as an explanation.

**Weaknesses.**

The "same person changed his mind" proof is read by the documents as evidence the threshold is undefined. There's an equally available reading: Webb made a real-time perceptual error under full match speed and corrected it with the benefit of slow-motion replay. That is an epistemic/measurement gap (live viewing vs. replay), not a definitional gap. If the rule said "studs contact above the sternum = automatic red," Webb's live and retrospective judgments would likely have converged, because the ambiguity wouldn't be in the definition, it would be in what he could perceive at full speed. This is a confound the project does not currently distinguish from the genuine "undefined term" cases, and it is the single most exploitable rebuttal against this incident specifically.

Three response options (no card / yellow / red) breaks the clean binary the rest of the mechanic relies on, adding friction to what should be the fastest decision in the demo.

**Could a judge reasonably reject this incident? Yes**, by reframing it as a live-vs-replay perception problem rather than a definitional one — which is a strictly different (and less interesting, for this project's purposes) claim than "the law has no boundary."

### 3. VAR "Clear and Obvious Error" (Judgment)

**Strengths.** Conceptually the sharpest: the correction mechanism's own activation criterion is undefined. This is the incident most likely to make a sophisticated judge say "huh."

**Weaknesses.**

This is also the incident most likely to make a *more* sophisticated judge say "this isn't new." The claim — that a standard requiring judgment to determine whether something is "clear" is inherently indeterminate at its boundary — is a well-known idea in legal philosophy (the open-texture problem: every rule has a settled core and an unsettled edge, and deciding which side of the edge a case falls on always requires judgment). A judge with any legal or philosophy background doesn't need football to already know this. The risk isn't that the point is wrong; it's that it isn't football-specific, and the project's own moat argument depends on specificity-as-differentiation. If the audience recognizes the underlying structure as general jurisprudence wearing a jersey, the "this is unique and irreplicable" claim weakens considerably — not because the football framing is false, but because the insight transfers so cleanly to any rule system that it stops feeling like a discovery about football law specifically.

The project's own scoring matrix already concedes this is the weakest incident on memorability, visual clarity, and emotional impact of the five selected (64, the lowest score among the chosen set). The documents' own fix — instantiate it through Perišić rather than presenting it standalone — is the right instinct, but it means this "incident" doesn't actually function as an independent fifth experience. It's a second pass over incident #1.

**Could a judge reasonably reject this incident? Yes, most easily of the five** — both for genericness (it restates a known idea in legal theory rather than discovering something football-specific) and because the project's own numbers already say it's the weakest standalone experience.

### 4. Mbappé "Deliberately Played" (Scope)

**Strengths.** The cleanest "recursive undefined" structure of the five — the definition's own definition is undefined. Genuinely under-discussed compared to the World Cup incidents, which gives it a "fresh discovery" quality the more famous incidents lack.

**Weaknesses.**

Lowest memorability score of the selected five (6) by the project's own matrix, and for good reason: it requires the most setup of any incident (explain the offside law, then explain the deliberate-play exception to the offside law, then explain the guidance criteria for the exception, before the user can even form an opinion). That is a lot of explanation to deliver before the "decision" — which is in some tension with the project's own Principle 2 ("Experience Before Explanation"). If the explanation needed to make the question intelligible is substantial, the experience-before-explanation sequencing is already compromised before the reveal even happens.

There is no injustice, no violence, no iconic image, no eliminated team. Of the five, this is the only incident with genuinely low emotional stakes — it is an intellectually elegant case riding on a low-drama event (a Nations League Final, not even a World Cup).

**Could a judge reasonably reject this incident? Yes** — "too much setup for too little payoff" is a fair characterization, and it's close to what the project's own scoring already says.

---

### 5. Suárez vs. Ghana (Context)

**Strengths.** The most famous and most morally charged incident of the five. The closing insight — the law was followed perfectly and the outcome was still felt as unjust — requires no rules literacy to land, which makes it the most accessible incident to a non-expert judge.

**Weaknesses.**

This is not actually an undefined-term case, and the stress-test document concedes this directly: there is no specific word in Law 12 that is ambiguous here. The Rulebook perspective in the signature-moment design doesn't disagree with anything — it simply states there is no remedy for the situation. That is an absence, not a disputed interpretation, and the project's own Principle 4 ("genuine divergence... each citing different legitimate evidence") and Principle 3 ("the undefined term... is the proof mechanism") both implicitly assume there is a term to dispute. By the project's own stated test, this incident is the weakest fit for its own framework — not an external critique, an internal inconsistency. It is being used as the emotional climax of the entire demo while being the one incident that least satisfies the demo's own definition of proof.

There's a second-order risk here too: "the law produces correct procedure but not necessarily a just outcome" is, like the VAR Judgment point, a generic observation about rule-based systems (the gap between legal positivism and substantive justice), not a football-specific one. It lands emotionally because the story is good, not because the legal-philosophy point is novel.

**Could a judge reasonably reject this incident? Yes, on the project's own stated criteria** — and this is the most damaging possible rejection, because it isn't an external attack, it's an internal contradiction a careful judge can find by reading the project's own principles document against its own incident registry.

## TASK 2 — HIDDEN FAILURE MODES

### Incident risks

**Requires too much football knowledge.** Mbappé/García is the worst offender — it requires knowing the offside law, the deliberate-play exception, and a relatively obscure 2021 match. De Jong requires recognizing Xabi Alonso and Howard Webb by name for full weight, though the visual (studs to chest) carries some of the load independent of names.

**Requires too much legal explanation before the decision.** VAR Judgment is the worst here: the user needs the four-category VAR scope, the "minimal interference, maximum benefit" philosophy, and the clear-and-obvious threshold explained before the question even makes sense. Mbappé is close behind for the reasons above.

**May confuse a non-football judge.** Mbappé and VAR Judgment both. A genuinely non-football audience member has no instinctive, confident first answer to either question — and the entire mechanic depends on the user having a confident wrong answer to destabilize. A user with no priors to destabilize doesn't experience the reveal as a discovery; they experience it as a lecture.

**May not create emotional impact.** VAR Judgment (lowest self-scored emotional impact of the five) and Mbappé (no injustice, no violence, no eliminated team, no iconic image) are both genuinely flat relative to the other three.

### Thesis risks

**Overreach.** The thesis, pushed to its logical conclusion, is true of nearly every natural-language rule system — contracts, statutes, regulations all have boundary terms that require judgment to apply. Football is the costume, not necessarily the unique substance of the claim. The stress test's defense ("specificity is the proof") answers a *vagueness* attack (is this claim too fuzzy to be meaningful) but does not answer a *genericness* attack (is this claim, however specific, actually a football-specific discovery, or a well-known general truth applied to football). These are different objections, and the existing defense only covers one of them.

**Becomes academic.** The Suárez "gap vs. term" distinction and the VAR "judgment about a judgment" recursion are intellectually sharp but cerebral. Both land in the middle-to-late part of the sequence, which is exactly where demo attention is most likely to slip — the structure risks losing emotional momentum to abstraction at the two points where it most needs to hold it.

**Becomes repetitive.** The mechanic — decide, see perspectives, see a word has no definition, decide again — is identical five times. By incident three, a user already knows the shape of the reveal before it happens. What should be five discoveries risks becoming one discovery and four confirmations, which weakens "experience before explanation" precisely because the explanation is now predictable in advance.

**Stops being surprising.** Following from the above: novelty is a depletable resource the current sequencing spends without tracking. The signature-moment document optimizes incident order for escalating *emotional intensity* but does not address declining *novelty* — and these are not the same curve. The fifth incident (Suárez) needs to feel like the biggest reveal of the five, but it arrives after the reveal format has already been seen four times.
