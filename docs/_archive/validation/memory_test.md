# Memory Test — Phase 2B

The question isn't what the incident teaches. It's what survives in a judge's head a week later, after forty-nine other demos, with no notes in front of them. That's a much lower bar than "understood the thesis," and several of these incidents don't clear it.

---

**Perišić Handball**
What's left: *"VAR gave a World Cup Final penalty over a word — 'deliberately' — that isn't even defined in the rulebook."*
This survives well. It's concrete, quotable, and doesn't require remembering a mechanic, just one striking fact.

**De Jong Chest Kick**
What's left: *"That kick should've been a red card. The ref gave yellow, then years later admitted he got it wrong."*
Strong. Doesn't even need the legal framing to be repeatable — "the ref changed his mind about his own call" is a complete, sticky story on its own.

**VAR Clear and Obvious**
What's left, optimistically: *"Something about VAR needing to decide if a mistake was 'obvious enough' to fix."* Realistically, for most judges: nothing distinct — if anything survives, it's likely to get folded into the Perišić memory without separate attribution, since it was probably delivered as a second pass over the same incident.
This is the weakest memory by a wide margin, and the project's own materials already predict this in the scoring matrix.

**Mbappé Deliberate Play**
What's left, optimistically: *"A deflection counted as 'on purpose' so the offside didn't count."*
This is recoverable but fragile — it's the incident most likely to be misremembered rather than forgotten outright, because the mechanic itself (deliberate play resets offside) is non-obvious and easy to garble in recall. A judge repeating this incorrectly to a colleague is arguably worse for the "specificity is the proof" principle than a judge forgetting it cleanly, since a wrong specific fact does more reputational damage than no fact at all.

**Suárez vs. Ghana**
What's left: *"The guy who punched the ball off the goal line on purpose to stop a goal — got the red card, his team still won."*
Strongest memory of the five. It requires zero rules literacy, has a built-in moral charge, and resolves into a single clean sentence that doesn't lose anything important in the retelling.

---

**Strongest memory: Suárez vs. Ghana.** It is also, per the adversarial review, the incident most vulnerable to the "this doesn't actually fit your own definition of proof" critique — the most memorable incident and the most structurally exposed one are the same incident. That's worth sitting with: the thing most likely to be repeated to a colleague a week later is also the thing most likely to be picked apart by a sharp judge in the room.

**Weakest memory: VAR Clear and Obvious.** Confirmed by the project's own scoring, the implementation notes admitting it needs to borrow another incident's setup, and this independent memory-decay test arriving at the same conclusion by a different method.
