# Replacement Analysis — Phase 2B

## If one incident must be removed, which one?

**VAR "Clear and Obvious Error."** It scores lowest of the selected five on memorability, visual clarity, and emotional impact by the project's own matrix (64, the lowest of the chosen set). It cannot stand as an independent fifth experience without borrowing Perišić's setup — the implementation note in the registry effectively admits this. And it carries the highest genericness risk of the five: a judge with any legal-philosophy background can collapse it into "this is just the open-texture problem in legal interpretation, not a football-specific discovery," which is a harder attack to recover from in real time than any of the other incidents face.

## Searching the incident universe for a stronger replacement

The Judgment category has only two candidates in the full eighteen-incident universe: #16 (the systemic VAR pattern, already selected) and #18 (France–Senegal, 2026). #18 scores lower across the board (55 vs. 64) and is explicitly flagged in the registry as too recent to have established documentation or consensus. Swapping #16 for #18 is a downgrade, not an upgrade — there is no stronger same-category replacement available.

Loosening the "one incident per category" constraint and asking whether anything else in the universe could fill the slot with a better story: the two strongest remaining incidents by total score are Zidane Headbutt (69) and Robben vs. Mexico (65), both already evaluated and rejected for stated reasons in the registry. Neither is a clean upgrade over VAR Judgment on the dimension that actually matters for this slot — legal-ambiguity purity, where VAR Judgment scores a 10, the highest of any incident in the universe alongside Mbappé. Swapping in Zidane or Robben would trade the project's single highest-scoring "pure undefined term" proof for an incident with better optics and a weaker, more contested claim to thesis purity. That is a real trade a strategist could choose to make, but it is a trade-off, not a strict improvement, and the task instructions are explicit: do not replace unless genuinely stronger.

**Finding: no incident in the provided universe is a genuinely stronger replacement for VAR Judgment in its category.** The category itself is structurally rare — there is essentially one real-world instantiation of "the correction mechanism's own threshold is undefined" at this level of fame, and it's the one already selected.

## What should actually change, if not the incident

The honest fix here is not a swap, it's a structural decision the documents have left unresolved: is VAR Judgment a standalone fifth two-decision cycle, or a second layer nested inside the Perišić incident? The registry lists it as one of "the final five" with its own full write-up, which reads as a standalone experience. The implementation note in the same document recommends instantiating it through Perišić specifically because it can't carry its own weight. Those two things can't both be true in the build. Committing to the nested version (Perišić as Intent question, then a second reveal turning the same incident into the Judgment question) costs little additional demo time and uses the incident's actual strength — recursion off an already-established emotional moment — instead of asking it to generate its own.

## The more honest removal candidate, if time forces a cut

If the real constraint is a three-minute demo running five full decide–evidence–reveal–decide cycles (which is a lot of content regardless of incident quality), **Mbappé is the more removable incident in practice**, not VAR Judgment. It carries the lowest memorability score and the highest setup cost of the five selected, and unlike VAR Judgment it has no natural way to nest inside another incident's narrative — removing it doesn't cost the project its most distinctive insight, it costs an intellectually elegant but emotionally flat side case. This isn't a "replace" recommendation in the sense the task asked for (no stronger incident takes its place), but it's the more defensible cut if forced to choose one.
