# Category Framework — Phase 2C

**Status:** CANONICAL. This document defines the final ambiguity category structure for the project.

---

## CATEGORY AUDIT

### Evaluation Criteria

Each category was evaluated on four dimensions:
1. **Distinctiveness** — Does this category prove the thesis through a mechanism no other category uses?
2. **Judge Clarity** — Can a non-expert judge understand what this category means within 10 seconds?
3. **Overlap Risk** — Does this category bleed into another category in ways that could confuse or undermine the taxonomy?
4. **Strength of Proof** — How powerfully does this category's incident demonstrate that the controversy is caused by legal architecture, not missing data?

---

## CATEGORY-BY-CATEGORY ASSESSMENT

### 1. INTENT — ✅ STRONG (with modification)

**What it means:** The law requires determining a mental state that no camera can observe.

**Distinctiveness:** High. No other category addresses unobservable internal states. The claim — "you cannot photograph a thought" — is immediately comprehensible and specific to intent terms.

**Judge Clarity:** Highest of the five. "Did the player mean to do it?" is a question every human being has engaged with. Zero football literacy required.

**Overlap Risk:** REAL. The adversarial review correctly identified that the Perišić incident — the flagship Intent case — involves both "deliberately" (intent) and "unnaturally bigger" (threshold). This is not a category weakness; it's a category assignment problem. The category itself is clean. The incident crosses categories.

**Resolution:** Perišić is reclassified as an Intent/Threshold *blend*, with Intent as the primary label because "deliberately" is the operative legal term in Law 12 and the one most cited in the controversy. The category definition remains pure. The incident assignment acknowledges impurity.

**Strength of Proof:** 10/10. "The word 'deliberately' appears four times in Law 12's handling section and is never defined anywhere in the Laws of the Game." This is a verifiable, quotable, specific fact.

---

### 2. THRESHOLD — ✅ STRONGEST

**What it means:** The law requires a severity judgment with no defined boundary between adjacent categories.

**Distinctiveness:** Highest of the five. The "same person, two conclusions" proof is unique to this category — it eliminates inter-rater variance and isolates the definitional gap as the sole cause of disagreement.

**Judge Clarity:** Very high. "Where does yellow end and red begin?" is instantly comprehensible. The visual (studs to chest) requires no setup.

**Overlap Risk:** Moderate. Threshold shares conceptual territory with Intent when the Perišić incident is considered (see above). As long as Perišić is framed as a blend and De Jong is presented as the *pure* Threshold case, the distinction holds: Intent asks "did they mean to?" while Threshold asks "how bad was it?"

**Resolution:** No change to category. Overlap with Intent is managed at the incident level (Perišić labeling), not the category level.

**Strength of Proof:** 10/10. The referee-reversed-himself evidence is the single most powerful proof in the entire set.

---

### 3. JUDGMENT — ⚠️ VALID BUT STRUCTURALLY DEPENDENT

**What it means:** The law defers entirely to subjective official assessment, and the correction mechanism designed to reduce that subjectivity uses an undefined term to define its own activation threshold.

**Distinctiveness:** High. No other category addresses the recursive problem — a judgment about whether another judgment was wrong enough to correct. This is the thesis's most elegant proof.

**Judge Clarity:** Lowest of the five. "Clear and obvious error" is a concept that requires understanding what VAR does, what its intervention threshold is, and why a threshold for correcting errors is itself an error-prone judgment. This is not instant comprehension — it requires 2-3 sentences of setup.

**Overlap Risk:** The adversarial review correctly identified a *genericness* risk, not an overlap risk. The observation that "a correction mechanism's activation standard is itself a judgment" is recognizable as the open-texture problem in legal philosophy. The risk is not that it overlaps with another project category — it's that a legally educated judge recognizes it as a well-known general observation, not a football-specific discovery.

**Resolution:** Category retained. Genericness risk mitigated by the football-specific defense: "VAR is the only billion-dollar system in any domain deployed specifically to correct interpretive errors, whose own activation criterion is an interpretive term. That is not a generic observation — it is a specific fact about the most-watched sport on earth."

**Structural commitment:** This category is delivered as a nested layer inside the Perišić incident, not as a standalone experience. See `reconciled_incident_registry.md`.

**Strength of Proof:** 9/10. Deducted 1 point for genericness risk and structural dependency on another incident.

---

### 4. SCOPE — ✅ VALID, UNIQUE CONTRIBUTION

**What it means:** The term that defines the law's area of application is itself undefined, and its own guidance criteria are also undefined — producing recursive undefined terms.

**Distinctiveness:** Unique. No other category demonstrates recursive undefined terms — where the definition's definition is itself undefined. "Undefined all the way down" is a structurally distinct proof mechanism.

**Judge Clarity:** Lowest standalone clarity. Requires understanding the offside law, the deliberate-play exception, and the guidance criteria before the ambiguity becomes visible. However, the decision question ("Is Mbappé offside?") requires no knowledge — the complexity arrives in the evidence phase, which preserves Experience Before Explanation.

**Overlap Risk:** Low. Scope could theoretically overlap with Intent (both involve the word "deliberately"), but the proof mechanism is completely different: Intent asks whether a mental state is observable; Scope asks whether a definitional boundary bottoms out in anything concrete. These are structurally distinct.

**Resolution:** Category retained. Its weakness (explanation overhead, low emotional stakes) is a *demo* problem, not a *category* problem. The category is valid and unique. The incident is the only viable representative.

**Strength of Proof:** 8/10. Intellectually strongest recursive proof, but the low emotional stakes and high setup cost reduce its impact in a timed demo.

---

### 5. CONTEXT — ✅ VALID, BUT OPERATES VIA DIFFERENT MECHANISM

**What it means:** The law produces a correct procedural outcome that fails to meet the situation's moral demand, and the law deliberately provides no concept for evaluating this gap.

**Distinctiveness:** High. No other category addresses the gap between procedural correctness and substantive justice. This is not about an undefined *word* — it is about an undefined *concept* that the law deliberately omits.

**Judge Clarity:** Highest emotional clarity. "Was justice served?" requires no rules knowledge, no football literacy, no legal framing. It is the most universally accessible question in the entire set.

**Overlap Risk:** The adversarial review correctly identified a *proof-mechanism mismatch*, not an overlap. Context does not overlap with another category — it operates via a fundamentally different proof mechanism:

| Categories 1–4 | Category 5 |
|:--------------|:-----------|
| Prove: "the law uses a term with no definition" | Proves: "the law says nothing about a question everyone expects it to answer" |
| Mechanism: **undefined term** | Mechanism: **deliberate silence** |

These are related but distinct. The original strategy treated them as identical, which the adversarial review correctly identified as an internal inconsistency.

**Resolution:** Category retained with explicit reclassification. Context is now presented as operating via a second, distinct proof mechanism — "deliberate silence" — rather than being forced into the "undefined term" framework where it doesn't fit. This distinction is surfaced in the demo, not hidden.

**Strength of Proof:** 9/10 (as a proof of the thesis's *broader* claim). 6/10 if forced into the "undefined term" sub-claim. By reframing it as a second mechanism, its strength becomes additive rather than dilutive.

## OVERLAP AND CONFUSION MATRIX

| Category Pair | Overlap Risk | Confusion Risk | Resolution |
|:-------------|:------------|:--------------|:-----------|
| Intent ↔ Threshold | **MODERATE** | Low | Managed at incident level (Perišić labeled as blend), not category level |
| Intent ↔ Scope | Low | Low | Both use "deliberately" but via different proof mechanisms (mental state vs. recursive definition) |
| Threshold ↔ Judgment | Low | Low | Threshold is about severity boundaries; Judgment is about correction-mechanism activation |
| Judgment ↔ Scope | Low | **MODERATE** | Both involve "layered" or "recursive" structures. Distinction: Judgment is meta-procedural (judging a judgment); Scope is meta-definitional (defining a definition). May need one sentence in demo to distinguish. |
| Context ↔ All Others | **STRUCTURAL** | **MODERATE** | Context uses a different proof mechanism. If not explicitly acknowledged, a judge may notice the inconsistency. Reconciled by declaring two mechanisms openly. |

## UNNECESSARY CATEGORIES — ASSESSMENT

Are any categories unnecessary? Could the taxonomy be reduced?

| Option | Categories | Verdict |
|:-------|:----------|:--------|
| Keep all 5 | Intent, Threshold, Judgment, Scope, Context | ✅ **RECOMMENDED** — each proves the thesis via a distinct mechanism |
| Merge Intent + Threshold | "Definitional Ambiguity" | ❌ Loses the mental-state vs. severity-boundary distinction, which is the primary differentiator |
| Drop Judgment | 4 categories | ❌ Loses the most elegant proof (correction mechanism reproduces the problem) |
| Drop Scope | 4 categories | ❌ Loses the only recursive-undefined proof |
| Drop Context | 4 categories | ❌ Loses the emotional closer and the "deliberate silence" mechanism |
| Merge Judgment + Scope | "Meta-Ambiguity" | ❌ Collapses two distinct proofs (procedural recursion vs. definitional recursion) into a label too abstract for a judge to grasp |

**Conclusion:** All five categories survive. No category is unnecessary. No merge improves clarity.

## FINAL CATEGORY STRUCTURE

| # | Category | Proof Mechanism | One-Sentence Judge Summary | Strength |
|:--|:---------|:----------------|:--------------------------|:---------|
| 1 | **Intent** | Mental state is unobservable | "The law asked 'did he mean it?' — but you can't photograph a thought." | ★★★★★ |
| 2 | **Threshold** | Severity boundary is undefined | "The law says 'too much force' — but doesn't say how much is too much." | ★★★★★ |
| 3 | **Judgment** | Correction mechanism is self-referential | "VAR was built to fix bad calls — but 'bad enough to fix' is itself a judgment call." | ★★★★☆ |
| 4 | **Scope** | Definitions are recursively undefined | "The law defines 'offside' using 'deliberately played' — which it also doesn't define." | ★★★★☆ |
| 5 | **Context** | The law is deliberately silent | "Every rule was followed. The outcome was still unjust. The law has no word for that." | ★★★★★ |

**Two proof mechanisms, five categories:**
- **Mechanism A (Undefined Terms):** Intent, Threshold, Judgment, Scope
- **Mechanism B (Deliberate Silence):** Context

Both mechanisms prove the same thesis: football law is architecturally designed to require human judgment. They demonstrate this through different paths — undefined words vs. undefined concepts — which makes the thesis more robust, not less.
