### 1. INTENT (PRIMARY) / THRESHOLD (SECONDARY) — Perišić Handball (2018 World Cup Final)

**Match:** France vs. Croatia, World Cup Final, 2018
**FIFA Law:** Law 12, Section 1 — Handling the ball
**Undefined Terms:** "Deliberately" / "Unnaturally bigger"
**Ambiguity Type:** Intent–Threshold blend. The "deliberately" question is an intent judgment (mental state); the "unnaturally bigger" question is a threshold judgment (body-position boundary). Both are genuinely undefined. Both are genuinely in play.
**Outcome:** Penalty awarded via VAR → Griezmann scores → France leads 2-1 → France wins 4-2

#### Why It Survives

The adversarial review's strongest critique — that the real controversy was about "unnaturally bigger" (threshold), not "deliberately" (intent) — is partially correct. The public debate did center more on arm position than on mental state. But the original strategy is also correct: "deliberately" is the operative legal term in Law 12, and it has no definition. Both terms are undefined. Both are in dispute. The incident proves *both* categories simultaneously, which is a strength, not a weakness — as long as the framing acknowledges it.

**What changes:** The demo framing must not claim this is a "pure intent" case. It should present it as: "Two undefined terms, two different kinds of ambiguity, one incident. The law asked whether Perišić 'deliberately' handled — an intent question — and whether his arm made his body 'unnaturally bigger' — a threshold question. Neither term is defined."

The "VAR worked" alternative reading (adversarial point) is addressed by the project's own thesis: VAR provided more *data* but did not resolve the *interpretation*. The referee changed his mind after review — which proves that additional evidence changed nothing about the ambiguity of the terms. This counter should be built into the framing, not left for a judge to raise.

#### Emotional Payoff
Immediate visual comprehension → confident first judgment → destabilization when two undefined terms are revealed → the realization that certainty was built on words without definitions.

#### Thesis Payoff
The strongest single proof: complete data exists, controversy is 100% interpretive, the terms are provably undefined, and VAR's intervention demonstrated that more data does not resolve definitional ambiguity.

#### Demo Value
**HIGHEST.** Opens the demo. Establishes the mechanic. Fastest to comprehend. World Cup Final stakes. Also serves as the foundation for the nested VAR Judgment reveal (see Incident 3).

### 2. THRESHOLD — De Jong Chest Kick on Xabi Alonso (2010 World Cup Final)

**Match:** Netherlands vs. Spain, World Cup Final, 2010
**FIFA Law:** Law 12, Section 3 — Disciplinary action
**Undefined Term:** "Serious foul play" vs. "reckless" (the boundary between yellow and red)
**Ambiguity Type:** Threshold — where does one severity category end and the next begin?
**Outcome:** Yellow card given → Webb later admits it should have been red

#### Why It Survives

The adversarial review's critique — that Webb's reversal could be a live-vs-replay perception problem, not a definitional one — is the strongest attack on any incident in the set. It deserves a preemptive answer.

**The defense holds for two reasons:**

1. **The perception-gap argument doesn't apply.** Webb *saw* the challenge live. He described seeing studs to chest. He gave a yellow *because he judged it reckless, not excessive*. His retrospective reversal was not "I didn't see what happened and now I do." It was "I saw exactly what happened and I applied the threshold differently on reflection." That is a definitional inconsistency, not a perceptual one.

2. **The law itself confirms the gap.** "Excessive force" is defined only as "endangers the safety of an opponent" — which is a restatement, not a definition. The law provides no force measurement, no height threshold, no injury requirement. Even if Webb's reversal *were* partly perceptual, the absence of any defined boundary means the perception problem and the definition problem are inseparable.

**What changes:** The three-option decision (no card / yellow / red) stays. The adversarial concern about breaking the "clean binary" is overruled — the *point* of this incident is that the law has three categories with undefined boundaries between them. Compressing to two options would obscure the thesis.

**Preemptive defense to build in:** "This isn't about what Webb saw. He saw the same thing both times. This is about what the word 'excessive' means — and the law doesn't say."

#### Emotional Payoff
Visceral shock at the visual → confident "red card" judgment (most users) → discovery that the world's best referee gave yellow → discovery that the same referee reversed himself → the realization that the boundary between reckless and excessive doesn't formally exist.

#### Thesis Payoff
The single most powerful piece of evidence in the entire set: same human, same footage, two opposite conclusions. This eliminates inter-rater variance as an explanation and isolates the definitional gap as the cause.

#### Demo Value
**VERY HIGH.** Second in sequence. Escalates emotional intensity. The "referee reversed himself" fact is the single most quotable, judge-memorable piece of evidence across all five incidents.

### 3. JUDGMENT — VAR "Clear and Obvious Error" (Nested Inside Perišić)

**Instantiation:** The Perišić handball VAR review itself
**FIFA Law:** VAR Protocol — Intervention threshold
**Undefined Term:** "Clear and obvious error"
**Ambiguity Type:** Judgment — a judgment about whether another judgment was wrong enough to correct
**Outcome:** Systemic pattern; instantiated through the Perišić review

#### Why It Survives

The adversarial review and the original strategy *agree* this is the purest thesis proof. They also agree it cannot stand alone. The disagreement is about whether this weakness is fatal.

**Ruling: It is not fatal, because the nested structure resolves it.**

The adversarial review correctly identifies that this is a restatement of the open-texture problem in legal philosophy — not a football-specific discovery. The original stress test's defense (specificity) answers a *vagueness* attack but not a *genericness* attack. These are different objections.

**The reconciled defense against genericness:** "Yes, this is a known property of rule-based systems. The football-specific insight is not that undefined terms exist in rules — it's that VAR was introduced *specifically to solve this problem* and the solution's own activation criterion reproduces the same problem. Football is the only domain where a billion-dollar technology was deployed to correct interpretive errors, and the technology's own operating standard — 'clear and obvious' — is itself an interpretive term. That is not a generic observation about rules. That is a specific, documented, verifiable fact about the most-watched sport on earth."

**What changes — STRUCTURAL COMMITMENT:**

VAR Judgment is **not** a standalone fifth decide-reveal-decide cycle. It is a **second reveal layer** inside the Perišić incident. The demo flow:

1. Perišić presented as Intent/Threshold question → Decision 1 → Evidence → Perspectives → Undefined-term reveal ("deliberately" / "unnaturally bigger") → Decision 2
2. **Immediately after:** "The referee initially said no penalty. VAR told him to look again. But VAR is only allowed to intervene for a 'clear and obvious error.' Was the referee's original no-penalty call 'clearly and obviously' wrong?" → Brief reveal that "clear and obvious" has no definition → Compressed insight about the correction mechanism reproducing the problem it was built to solve

This costs ~20-30 additional seconds, not a full additional cycle. It leverages emotional momentum from Perišić rather than generating its own. And it produces the single most intellectually elegant moment in the demo — the recursive loop where the technology designed to solve ambiguity introduces new ambiguity.

**One-line preemptive defense to build into the reveal:** "Every rule system has terms that require judgment. But football spent billions deploying VAR specifically to fix that problem — and VAR's own activation standard is one of those terms."

#### Emotional Payoff
Not a standalone emotional beat. It is the "oh, wait" extension of the Perišić reveal — a second cognitive shift that deepens the first. The user goes from "the word is undefined" to "the system built to fix undefined words uses an undefined word to operate."

#### Thesis Payoff
The most elegant proof: the correction mechanism's activation threshold is itself undefined. This is the thesis at its most recursive and its most devastating. It demonstrates that adding technology does not remove the judgment requirement — it relocates it.

#### Demo Value
**HIGH when nested; LOW if standalone.** This is now committed to nested delivery. It does not appear in the demo as a separate incident. It appears as the final 20-30 seconds of the Perišić sequence.

### 4. SCOPE — Mbappé "Deliberate Play" Offside Reset (2021 Nations League Final)

**Match:** France vs. Spain, UEFA Nations League Final, 2021
**FIFA Law:** Law 11 — Offside / "Deliberately played"
**Undefined Term:** "Deliberately played" (as distinguished from deflection)
**Ambiguity Type:** Scope — the term that defines the law's area of application is itself undefined, and its own guidance terms are also undefined
**Outcome:** Goal awarded to Mbappé — García's touch ruled as "deliberate play" resetting offside

#### Why It Survives

The adversarial review is correct that this incident has the highest explanation overhead and the lowest emotional stakes of the five. It is the most vulnerable to the "too much setup for too little payoff" critique.

**It survives for two reasons:**

1. **No replacement exists.** The Scope category has only three candidates (#14 Argentina Subs, #15 Mbappé, #17 Lautaro). Argentina Subs is weaker on every dimension. Lautaro was resolved by technology. Mbappé is the only viable Scope incident.

2. **"Undefined all the way down" is a unique proof mechanism.** No other incident in the set demonstrates recursive undefined terms — where the definition's own definition is itself undefined. This is a structurally distinct contribution that no other incident provides. Cutting it would mean the project demonstrates four proof mechanisms instead of five, reducing the taxonomy from a system to a list.

**What changes:** The sequencing recommendation in signature_moment_design.md placed Mbappé 3rd. This is confirmed as correct — it belongs in the middle of the sequence, after emotional momentum is established (Perišić, De Jong) and before the meta-insight (VAR nested reveal) and emotional climax (Suárez). The middle position protects it: the audience has bought in by this point, and the intellectual elegance of "undefined all the way down" can carry a lower-energy stretch.

**Explanation overhead mitigation:** The decision question ("Is Mbappé offside?") requires no rules knowledge — any viewer can answer based on position. The *explanation* of the deliberate-play exception comes *after* Decision 1 as part of the evidence phase. This preserves Experience Before Explanation (Principle 2). The user decides first, then learns why their decision is more complicated than they thought.

#### Emotional Payoff
Lower than the other four. Compensated by intellectual satisfaction — the "it's undefined all the way down" realization is the demo's most philosophically elegant moment. Not every beat needs to be emotional; this one is cerebral by design.

#### Thesis Payoff
Unique contribution: demonstrates recursive undefined terms. The scope of the law depends on a term whose own guidance criteria are themselves undefined. This is the only incident that proves the law's definitions never "bottom out" in anything concrete.

#### Demo Value
**MODERATE.** Third in sequence. Requires the most setup but delivers a structurally unique insight. Protected by position — audience momentum carries it.

### 5. CONTEXT — Suárez Goal-Line Handball vs. Ghana (2010 World Cup Quarter-Final)

**Match:** Uruguay vs. Ghana, World Cup Quarter-Final, 2010
**FIFA Law:** Law 12 — Handling / DOGSO; Law 5 — Advantage
**Undefined Element:** The law's deliberate silence on contextual adequacy of prescribed punishment
**Ambiguity Type:** Context — the law produces a correct procedural outcome that fails to match the situation's moral demand, and the law deliberately provides no framework for evaluating this gap
**Outcome:** Red card + penalty correctly applied → Ghana misses penalty → Uruguay advances

#### Why It Survives

The adversarial review's critique is the most structurally dangerous in the entire review: Suárez is not an undefined-*term* case. There is no ambiguous word in Law 12 here. The law was correctly applied. The controversy is about the adequacy of the remedy, not the meaning of a term. By the project's own Principle 3 ("Specificity Is the Proof") and Principle 4 (genuine divergence citing the undefined term), Suárez is the weakest fit for the project's own framework.

**And it must stay.**

The adversarial review is right on the structural point. The original strategy is right on the strategic point. Suárez is the strongest closing beat in the entire set. It requires zero football literacy. It produces the strongest post-demo memory. It generates the most visceral moral reaction. Removing it would be structurally tidier and strategically suicidal.

**The reconciled solution is honesty-as-strategy:**

Suárez does not prove the same claim as the other four. The other four prove: "the law uses a term with no definition." Suárez proves: "the law deliberately says nothing about whether its remedies are adequate to every context." These are related but distinct mechanisms by which the law preserves space for human judgment:

1. **Undefined terms** — words that carry legal weight with no formal definition (Perišić, De Jong, VAR, Mbappé)
2. **Deliberate silence** — concepts the law could address but intentionally doesn't (Suárez)

Both are architectural features, not bugs. Both demonstrate that the law was designed to require judgment. But they operate differently, and the demo should say so.

**What changes — CRITICAL REFRAME:**

The Suárez reveal should explicitly present this as a second proof mechanism:

> *"The first four incidents showed you terms the law uses but doesn't define. This incident is different. This is about something the law doesn't say at all. There is no term for 'adequate punishment.' No framework for whether a penalty kick — a 75% chance at a goal — is a sufficient remedy for preventing a 100% certain goal. The law is deliberately silent on this question. Not because the lawmakers forgot. Because they chose not to answer it."*

This reframe turns the structural weakness into the demo's most sophisticated moment. A judge who notices the category mismatch hears it acknowledged — which reads as intellectual honesty, not as a seam in the argument. A judge who doesn't notice it simply experiences the most emotionally powerful moment in the demo.

#### Emotional Payoff
**HIGHEST of the five.** "The law was followed perfectly and the outcome was still felt as unjust" requires no rules knowledge, generates universal moral response, and produces the single most memorable sentence in the entire demo. This is what a judge carries out of the room.

#### Thesis Payoff
Proves the thesis's second mechanism: the law preserves judgment not only through undefined terms but through deliberate silence on questions everyone expects it to answer. This elevates the thesis from "the law has some vague words" to "the law is architecturally designed to be incomplete."

#### Demo Value
**HIGHEST.** Closes the demo. Final beat. The thing the judge remembers a week later: "The law was followed exactly. Ghana still went home. The law doesn't define justice."
