## Per-Project Verdicts (assuming full, polished execution)

### 1. FirstTouch
**Why it wins:** It's the only project that proves its own honesty mechanically — the Truth Anchor vetoing the model against real geometry — instead of asking anyone to trust it. Every judge role independently likes this for a different reason: IBM judge sees real MCP/LangChain orchestration, Technical judge sees a verifiable architecture, Product judge sees a coherent four-tab narrative arc (Decision → Profile → Consequence → What-If), Startup judge sees an obvious buyer (broadcasters, clubs, scouting departments).
**Why it loses:** It's cold. Expected threat valuation and Decision DNA radars are a vocabulary, not a story — a judge who isn't already analytically inclined has to be sold on caring before they're impressed by the rigor.
**Biggest strength:** Verifiable grounding. Nobody else in this field can say "the model is structurally prevented from hallucinating outside what the data shows" and mean it.
**Biggest weakness:** No emotional hook independent of the rigor — if a judge doesn't engage with the analytics, there's nothing else carrying the demo.

### 2. Inside the Room
**Why it wins:** Most original thesis in the field, by a wide margin, and the only project whose closing claim ("AI's job is to expose discretion, not eliminate it") maps directly onto where IBM's actual current governance/trust messaging sits. Done well, it's the only demo in the room that argues something about AI deployment generally, not just about football.
**Why it loses:** It's the only project that requires the judge to follow an argument before the payoff lands. Everyone else's "wow" is visual or immediate; yours is conceptual and arrives in minute two, not minute one. A tired judge on project fourteen of the day gives FirstTouch's Truth Anchor ten seconds of attention and gets it instantly. They give your thesis the same ten seconds and might not.
**Biggest strength:** Category ownership — nobody else in this lineup can be repositioned into "discretion-risk governance" without becoming a different product.
**Biggest weakness:** Legibility under time pressure. The idea is the strongest asset in the room and the hardest one to transmit in a 3-minute slot against five other demos competing on instant impact.

### 3. DECODED
**Why it wins:** Breadth as armor. Five modules means a tired judge who only samples two of them still leaves thinking "they built a lot" — that's a real scoring effect, not a flaw in judge behavior, and it reliably produces solid-not-spectacular scores across every rubric line.
**Why it loses:** Ask any judge "what's the one thing this proves" and you get five different answers depending which module they happened to open. A panel discussing finalists afterward struggles to summarize it in one sentence — and the project that doesn't compress doesn't get argued for in the room when prize money is actually being decided.
**Biggest strength:** Coverage. It never embarrasses itself by being thin on any single rubric dimension.
**Biggest weakness:** No spine. It's five competent apps, not one argument — sophisticated panels notice this exact pattern and actively discount for it once spotted.

### 4. PitchSense AI
**Why it wins:** The most honest, lowest-risk submission in the field — disclosed time-based train/test split, no overclaiming, does exactly what it says. A risk-averse judge trusts it completely.
**Why it loses:** Trust isn't the same as excitement. It's the project least likely to generate a single sentence anyone repeats walking out of the room. Competent and forgettable in the same breath.
**Biggest strength:** Methodological integrity — zero attack surface for a skeptical technical judge.
**Biggest weakness:** Low ceiling. Even flawless execution caps out at "solid," because the underlying idea (predictor + explainer chatbot) is the median submission for this exact challenge.

### 5. Pitch-Vision AI
**Why it wins:** First ten seconds, nobody beats it. ECG biometrics, 2.5D telestrator, agent topology animation — instant visceral "wow" that lands before any judge has formed a critical opinion.
**Why it loses:** The wow doesn't survive a second question. "What's the one idea this proves" has no answer — it's a feature collection wearing a thesis-shaped README, and any judge who pushes past the surface finds nothing underneath holding the five features together.
**Biggest strength:** Production value as pure spectacle — unmatched in this field on visual impact alone.
**Biggest weakness:** Zero throughline. Polish without an argument is the textbook profile of a project that scores high on "demo impact" and gets quietly passed over when prize money decisions get made in committee.

### 6. Dallas World Cup Ecosystem
**Why it wins:** Real, concrete economic value for a real audience (local businesses, visitors) — the least abstract pitch in the room, easiest for a non-technical judge to immediately grasp "who this helps and how."
**Why it loses:** This is the weakest fit for an AI Builders Challenge specifically. It's a content-and-information-orchestration platform, not a reasoning system — there's no comparable depth of AI-native logic to what Granite is doing in any of the other five projects. An IBM judge and a Technical judge both ask the same question here: "where's the hard AI problem being solved?" and the honest answer is "mostly logistics and content generation," which doesn't compete on this rubric's actual axis.
**Biggest strength:** Tangible, near-term business utility — the most "ready to deploy tomorrow" project in the field.
**Biggest weakness:** Almost no technical or conceptual depth relative to the other five. It's playing a different game in a competition scored on AI sophistication.

FirstTouch 85% · Inside the Room 70% · DECODED 65% · PitchSense AI 35% · Pitch-Vision AI 30% · Dallas World Cup Ecosystem 15%

**4. What keeps Inside the Room from #1:**
Not idea quality — it has the best thesis in the field by a clear margin, and that's not a hedge, it's the actual ranking driver putting it at #2 over four other projects. What keeps it from #1 is *time-to-payoff*. FirstTouch's strongest asset (the Truth Anchor) is legible in the first ten seconds, cold, with zero setup. Inside the Room's strongest asset (the thesis itself) only lands after the judge has sat through enough of the demo to understand what's being claimed. In a panel moving fast through finalists, the project that front-loads its proof beats the project with the better proof that arrives in minute two — every time, regardless of which is actually the smarter idea.

**5. The single change that would move it to #1:**
Move the Framing Sensitivity Test (showing Granite's reading shift with how an incident is phrased) to the literal opening fifteen seconds of the demo — before any incident walkthrough, before any thesis statement, before "Inside the Room" is even named. Open cold on two side-by-side Granite outputs for the same incident, two different phrasings, visibly different verdicts. That's a FirstTouch-grade instant "wow" — except it's also the strongest evidence for your thesis that exists, not just spectacle in front of an argument. Do that, and you've matched FirstTouch's first-ten-seconds legibility while keeping the deepest idea in the field. That combination is what actually wins a final round — not a better idea alone, and not a faster hook alone.

**VERDICT:** FirstTouch wins on current trajectory; Inside the Room is the only project that can realistically take #1, and only by fixing pacing, not substance.
**KEY INSIGHT:** In this field, the prize doesn't go to the best idea or the best hook in isolation — it goes to whichever project compresses its best idea into its hook fastest.
**MOST LIKELY MISTAKE:** Treating "the thesis is strong" as sufficient and leaving the framing-sensitivity proof buried mid-demo instead of leading with it.
**BIGGEST RISK:** A fast-moving panel never reaches minute two of your demo at all.
**BEST ALTERNATIVE:** If the cold-open framing-test can't be made bulletproof live, open on a pre-recorded 10-second clip of it instead of cutting it — the sequencing fix matters more than whether it's literally live in that exact instant.
**CONFIDENCE:** Medium-high on the relative ranking logic; medium on the exact percentages, since real judging panels weight criteria idiosyncratically and I don't have this panel's actual rubric.
